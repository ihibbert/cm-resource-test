Chapter 1.1 – Why so many Expats consider Thailand

    From stunning beaches to lush jungles and picturesque mountains, Thailand boasts a diverse range of natural beauty that appeals to many expats.
  3. Warm and welcoming culture:
    Thai people are known for their hospitality and friendliness, making it easy for expats to feel at home and build connections with locals.
  4. Accessible healthcare:
    Thailand has a well-developed healthcare system, with modern facilities and highly trained medical professionals offering affordable and high-quality care.
  5. Vibrant expat community:
    Many cities in Thailand, including Chiang Mai, have established expat communities that provide support, social connections, and opportunities for networking.
  6. Delicious food:
    Thai cuisine is renowned for its rich flavors and variety, offering expats a culinary adventure to enjoy.
  7. Ease of travel

Chapter 1.2 – Why consider Chiang Mai?

Chiang Mai: The Rose of the North

Chiang Mai is a vibrant city located in northern Thailand. It is renowned for its rich history, beautiful landscapes, and cultural heritage.

Location and Geography:

Chiang Mai is the second largest city in Thailand, with a population of approximately 1.3 million. It is known as The Rose of the North. Chiang Mai is situated in a mountainous region, surrounded by lush greenery and forests. The city is approximately 700 kilometres north of Bangkok and is the capital of the Chiang Mai Province.

Weather

Chiang Mai experiences a tropical climate, with three distinct seasons.

  1. The hot season (March to June): Temperatures range from approximately 30°C (86°F) to 38°C (100°F), with high humidity levels.
  2. The rainy season (July to October): Expect frequent rain showers and thunderstorms, with temperatures ranging from about 25°C (77°F) to 32°C (90°F).
  3. The cool season (November to February): This is the most comfortable time to visit Chiang Mai, with temperatures ranging from about 15°C (59°F) to 28°C (82°F) and less rainfall.

The weather in Chiang Mai can be unpredictable, so it is always a good idea to pack an umbrella or raincoat, especially during the rainy season!

History and Culture:

Lanna Kingdom Influence:
Chiang Mai was founded in 1296 as the capital of the Lanna Kingdom. The city has a strong historical connection to the kingdom, which is evident in its architecture, art, and traditions.

Temples and Religious Sites:
The city is dotted with numerous Buddhist temples (wats) that showcase exquisite architecture and craftsmanship. Wat Phra at Doi Suthep, located on a mountain overlooking the city, is one of the most famous temples in Thailand.

Festivals:
Chiang Mai hosts several vibrant festivals throughout the year, including the Yi Peng Lantern Festival (known for its beautiful floating lanterns) and the Songkran Water Festival (Thai New Year celebration).

Attractions and Activities:

Old City:
The historic Old City area is surrounded by a moat and remnants of ancient walls. It is a great place to explore temples, markets, and traditional Thai architecture.

Night Bazaar:
Chiang Mai's Night Bazaar is famous for its bustling atmosphere, street food, handicrafts, and souvenirs. It is an excellent place to shop and experience local culture.

Nature and Adventure:
Surrounding Chiang Mai are opportunities for trekking, visiting waterfalls, and exploring hill tribe villages. Elephant sanctuaries and zip-lining adventures are also popular activities.

Cooking Classes:
Chiang Mai offers fantastic opportunities to learn about Thai cuisine through cooking classes where you can visit local markets, learn to cook traditional dishes, and enjoy delicious meals.

Lifestyle and Atmosphere:

Chiang Mai has a relaxed and laid-back atmosphere compared to bustling cities like Bangkok. It is a popular destination for digital nomads and expats due to its affordable cost of living, pleasant climate, and the availability of modern amenities.

Cuisine:
The city boasts a diverse culinary scene. In addition to traditional Thai cuisine, you will find a variety of international restaurants, trendy cafes, and street food stalls serving delicious dishes at all price points.

Summary

Chiang Mai is a fascinating city that blends history, culture, and natural beauty. Whether you are interested in exploring ancient temples, experiencing local festivals, or immersing yourself in nature, Chiang Mai offers a unique and memorable travel experience in Thailand.

Chapter 1.4 – As a potential expat, what do you need to know about Visas?

Visas and Entry Requirements for Thailand

Entering Thailand as a tourist for a period of 30 days without a visa is relatively easy for people from countries such as the United States, most of the European Union, Australia, and others. (Check the Thai Immigration website for the latest info)

a. Tourist Visas:

  1. Visa on Arrival (VOA)
    • Available for citizens of certain countries who arrive at designated international airports in Thailand.
    • Allows a stay of up to 15-30 days for tourism purposes.
  2. Tourist Visa (TR)
    • Valid for tourism purposes and generally allows stays of up to 60 days per entry.
    • Can be extended for an additional 30 days at a Thai immigration office.
  3. Multiple Entry Tourist Visa (METV)
    • Allows multiple entries into Thailand over a period of up to 6 months.
    • Each entry allows a stay of up to 60 days.

b. Non-Immigrant Visas (for specific purposes):

  1. Non-Immigrant Visa Category “B” (Business Visa)
    • For individuals planning to work or conduct business in Thailand.
    • Requires sponsorship from a Thai company or employer.
  2. Non-Immigrant Visa Category “ED” (Education Visa)
    • Intended for students enrolled in educational institutions in Thailand.
    • Issued for the duration of the course of study.
  3. Non-Immigrant Visa Category “O” (Other purposes)
    • Includes several sub-categories such as retirement, marriage, accompanying spouse or child, etc.
    • Requires appropriate documentation depending on the purpose.

Visa Exemptions:

  1. Visa Exemption Scheme
    • Citizens of certain countries are exempt from obtaining a visa when entering Thailand for tourism purposes for a limited duration (typically 30-90 days, depending on nationality).
    • The exempt period can be extended by applying at a Thai immigration office.

Long-Term Stays:

  1. Retirement Visa (Non-Immigrant “O-A” Visa)
    • For individuals aged 50 or above who wish to retire in Thailand.
    • Requires proof of retirement funds and other documentation.
  2. Elite Visa Program
    • Offers long-term residency visas with various benefits and privileges, such as concierge services and visa facilitation.
  3. Digital Nomads
    • Thailand has recently launched a new “Destination Thailand Visa” (DTV) specifically designed for digital nomads, allowing remote workers to stay in the country for up to five years.
    • Prior to this, digital nomads could only stay in Thailand for up to 60 days on a tourist visa, or obtain a difficult-to-get Long Term Residence Visa with steep financial requirements.
    • The DTV aims to boost visitor numbers, revive the economy, and attract more remote workers to Thailand.
    • To apply for the DTV, individuals must be self-employed or work for a company outside of Thailand, and meet certain requirements.
    • The application fee is 10,000 Thai baht (approximately 252 euros), and the processing fee for a 10-year visa is 50,000 Thai baht per person.
    • Overall, this new visa scheme is an exciting development for digital nomads looking to base themselves in Thailand and experience the country’s rich culture, beautiful scenery, and friendly people.

Work Permits:

  1. Work Permit
    • Required for foreign nationals working in Thailand.
    • Generally obtained in conjunction with a Non-Immigrant “B” Visa.

Special Cases:

  1. Diplomatic and Official Visas
    • Issued to diplomats, government officials, or individuals on official missions.
  2. Transit Visa
    • For individuals transiting through Thailand to a third destination.

Visa Extensions:

Regardless of visa type, many visas can be extended within Thailand at a Thai immigration office for additional periods, typically up to 30 days.

Note: It is important to note that visa requirements and regulations can change, so it is advisable to consult the nearest Thai embassy or consulate or visit the official website of the Thai Ministry of Foreign Affairs for the most up-to-date information before planning your trip or stay in Chiang Mai.

Chapter 1.5 – Getting to Chiang Mai

Getting to Chiang Mai is easy thanks to its well-connected transportation infrastructure. Here are the primary ways to reach Chiang Mai:

Air

  • Chiang Mai International Airport (CNX) is the main gateway to the city. A new airport is to be built and preparation is already underway.
  • Direct flights to Chiang Mai are available from major cities in Thailand (e.g., Bangkok, Phuket) as well as international destinations including Singapore, Hong Kong, Taipei, Kuala Lumpur, and more.
  • Airlines such as Thai Airways, Bangkok Airways, AirAsia, and others operate flights to Chiang Mai.

While most people arrive in Bangkok as their first Thai point of entry, there may be an advantage of flying directly to Chiang Mai – especially if you have lots of luggage. You may find it easier to clear customs in CM and avoid transferring your luggage to domestic carrier. The cost of your flight options is of course a primary concern as well.

Train

  • Chiang Mai is accessible by train from Bangkok.
  • The journey by train takes around 10-12 hours and offers scenic views of the Thai countryside.
  • Trains depart from Bangkok's Hua Lamphong Station (Bangkok Railway Station) to Chiang Mai Railway Station. NOTE: It is a real treat to take the overnight train between Bangkok and Chiang Mai! Each way departs about 6:00PM and arrives at about 7:00AM the next day. The seats convert to a bed. It is awesome!

Bus

  • Long-distance buses connect Chiang Mai with various cities across Thailand.
  • Buses from Bangkok to Chiang Mai take around 9-12 hours depending on the type of service (VIP, express, sleeper).
  • Several bus companies operate services to Chiang Mai, with departures from Bangkok's Northern Bus Terminal (Mo Chit) and other locations.

Car or Motorbike

  • Chiang Mai is accessible by road from other parts of Thailand.
  • The journey by car or motorbike from Bangkok takes approximately 8-10 hours, depending on traffic and route.
  • Major highways connect Chiang Mai with cities like Lampang, Chiang Rai, and Mae Hong Son.

Minivan

  • Minivans provide a faster alternative to buses for travelling between nearby cities and towns.
  • Minivans operate on various routes within northern Thailand, including Chiang Mai to Pai, Chiang Mai to Chiang Rai, and others.

Tips for Travelling to Chiang Mai

  • Book Flights in Advance: To get the best fares, especially during peak tourist seasons, book flights to Chiang Mai in advance.
  • Consider Overnight Trains or Buses: Overnight trains or buses from Bangkok to Chiang Mai can save time and accommodation costs. Note: These options may be particularly attractive if you are travelling with lots of luggage and wish to avoid airline baggage fees.
  • Choose Reputable Operators: When travelling by bus or minivan, choose reputable operators with good safety records.
  • Check Visa Requirements: Ensure you have the necessary visa or entry requirements for Thailand depending on your nationality.

Chiang Mai is a popular destination in Thailand with excellent transport links, making it accessible from various locations within the country and internationally.

Chapter 1.6 – A Little Cultural Advice

It is important to remember that we are visitors here. Thai culture has evolved here for many centuries. It is important to be mindful of our hosts’ culture and customs to show respect and appreciation for the local way of life. Thais are a very welcoming and friendly people (as well as being incredibly honest!). Here are some cultural tips and advice for navigating Chiang Mai's cultural landscape:

Respect for Buddhism

  • Thailand is 98% Buddhist, and temples (wats) hold significant cultural and religious importance.
  • When visiting temples, dress modestly (covering shoulders and knees) and remove shoes before entering temple buildings.
  • Show respect by not pointing your feet towards Buddha statues or monks. Do not touch the monks.

Greetings and Politeness

  • Use the traditional Thai greeting called the wai (placing palms together in a prayer-like gesture) when meeting locals, especially elders or those in positions of respect.
  • Address people respectfully using khun (equivalent to Mr. or Ms.) followed by their first name.

Modesty in Dress

  • While Chiang Mai is generally more relaxed compared to Bangkok, it is respectful to dress modestly, especially when visiting temples or in rural areas.
  • Avoid overly revealing clothing, particularly in religious or conservative settings.

Footwear Etiquette

  • It has been customary to remove shoes before entering someone's home, temples, or certain shops and establishments. Many still adhere to this custom. You will likely be provided a good clue by checking to see if there are shoe racks (or simply pairs of loose shoes) outside an establishment.

Cultural Sensitivity

  • Avoid public displays of affection, especially in conservative areas.
  • Refrain from touching a Thai person's head, as the head is considered sacred in Thai culture.
  • Be mindful of local customs and traditions, and observe how locals behave in different situations.

Eating and Dining Etiquette

  • When dining with others, wait for the host or elder to start eating before you begin.
  • Thais tend to use a spoon (not a fork) to eat most dishes, and use chopsticks for noodle dishes. It is not common to see table knives used here.
  • When at someone's home, try to finish all the food on your plate to show appreciation.

Language and Communication

  • Learn a few basic Thai phrases such as sawasdee kha/krab (hello), khob khun kha/krab (thank you), and khor tort (excuse me).
  • Speak calmly and avoid raising your voice, as Thais value harmony and politeness.

Respecting the Monarchy

  • The Thai monarchy is highly revered, and it is important to show respect towards the King and Royal Family.
  • Avoid any disrespectful comments or actions related to the monarchy, as this can be considered a serious - even criminal - offense in Thailand.

Tipping and Payments

  • Tipping is not mandatory but appreciated, especially in tourist areas or for exceptional service.
  • In restaurants, rounding up the bill or leaving a small tip (10-20 baht) is common practice.

Patience and Smiling

  • Thais appreciate patience, politeness, and a friendly demeanour.
  • Smile often and remain calm in all interactions, even if faced with language barriers or misunderstandings.

By embracing these cultural tips and demonstrating respect for Thai customs, you will create positive interactions and experiences while exploring Chiang Mai and immersing yourself in the local culture. Thais are generally welcoming and gracious hosts, and showing cultural sensitivity will help foster meaningful connections and enrich your time in this beautiful city.

  • Keep your original number By using an eSIM for data, you can continue to use your primary phone number for calls and texts via Wi-Fi-based messaging apps. This allows you to stay connected with your contacts without having to update your information or carry multiple devices.

  • Easy to manage You can switch between multiple eSIM profiles directly on your device, making it easier to manage your international data plans and switch providers if needed.

Overall, getting an eSIM before traveling abroad can offer convenience, cost savings, and flexibility while keeping you connected with your personal and professional networks.

Chapter 1.8 – What to do with your existing mobile phone number

Keeping your mobile phone number from your home country can be advantageous for expats in various ways. Firstly, it ensures that you maintain continuity in your personal and professional communications as you settle into your new life abroad. Your contacts will still be able to reach you at the same number, and you will not have to update your details on numerous platforms. Secondly, retaining your existing number allows you to keep using certain services that might not be easily accessible from foreign numbers or require local verification. Finally, maintaining your original phone number can help you stay connected with important institutions such as banks and government agencies, making it easier to manage affairs in your home country even while living abroad.

For those from the US, one can port one's mobile number to Google Voice essentially for free, and continue to receive voice messages and text messages to that number without monthly fees. This is especially helpful when one needs to receive Two Factor Authentication (2FA) texts from an account/bank that has one’s phone number associated with the account.

For people moving to Thailand from countries other than the US, there are still some options available that can provide similar functionality to Google Voice:

  • Skype Skype offers the "Skype Number" feature, which is available in many countries, including Australia, UK, Canada, and others. This feature allows you to receive calls and texts to a dedicated phone number.
  • Localphone Localphone provides virtual phone numbers for a variety of countries, so users from Australia, UK, Canada, and other countries can purchase a phone number from their home country and use it to receive calls and texts while in Thailand.
  • TextNow While TextNow primarily offers US phone numbers, they also provide Canadian phone numbers. Users from Canada can use TextNow to maintain their Canadian phone number while living in Thailand.

Please note that availability and features of these services may vary depending on the user’s home country.

Chapter 1.10 – Think about how to get local currency

There are several options for obtaining local currency (Thai Baht) in Thailand, each with its own advantages and disadvantages:

ATMs

  • Pros: Convenient, available at most banks, and generally offer competitive exchange rates.
  • Cons: Withdrawal fees can be high, and your home bank may also charge additional fees.

Currency Exchange Booths

  • Pros: Widely available in tourist areas, offer a variety of currencies for exchange, and often provide competitive rates.
  • Cons: Some exchange booths may have hidden fees or less favorable rates, so it is essential to compare prices before exchanging money.

Banks

  • Pros: Generally offer good exchange rates and a secure environment for exchanging currency.
  • Cons: May have longer wait times and limited opening hours compared to other options.

Using Credit/Debit Cards to get cash and/or for purchases

  • Pros: Offer convenience and often provide competitive exchange rates.
  • Cons: Some establishments may not accept foreign cards, and you may incur additional fees from your card issuer (Often call Foreign Transaction Fees). Do yourself a favor and check with the card issuer BEFORE you depart so you won’t have any surprises.

Travelers Checks

  • Pros: Offer security and can be replaced if lost or stolen.
  • Cons: Less convenient, as you will need to find a bank or exchange booth that accepts them, and exchange rates may be less favorable.

Online Currency Exchange Platforms

  • Pros: May offer competitive rates and can be more convenient for large amounts.
  • Cons: Some platforms may have limited availability or require you to wait for delivery, and there may be additional fees for the service.

Each option has its pros and cons, so it is best to consider your personal preferences, financial situation, and travel needs before deciding on the most suitable method to obtain local currency in Thailand.

Thailand celebrates a variety of public holidays throughout the year, including traditional Thai festivals, religious observances, and national holidays. Here is a list of some major Thai holidays that are observed across the country:

  • Lha Bucha Day (ฬหบูชา)

Chapter 3.2 – How to get there – Walking

Walking is a GREAT way to get around in Chiang Mai. You will notice much more detail about your surrounding than when driving. Crossing streets in Chiang Mai can be an adventure due to the city's busy traffic and the lack of formal pedestrian infrastructure in some areas. Here are some tips to help you cross streets safely in Chiang Mai:

  1. Use Designated Crosswalks/Pedestrian Crossings
    When available, always use designated crosswalks or pedestrian signals. However, be aware that not all drivers may stop for pedestrians, even at crosswalks.
  2. Find a Safe Spot
    If there are no crosswalks, find a spot where you have a clear view of the oncoming traffic in both directions. This allows you to gauge the speed and distance of vehicles more accurately.
  3. Follow Local Pedestrians
    Observe how locals cross the streets. They often have experience with the traffic patterns and can show you the best practices. Crossing with a group can also make drivers more likely to slow down.
  4. Make Eye Contact
    Try to make eye contact with approaching drivers to ensure they see you. This can also give you an indication of whether they intend to stop or slow down.
  5. Be Patient and Decisive
    Wait for a gap in the traffic before crossing. Once you decide to cross, do so confidently but remain vigilant. Hesitation can confuse drivers and increase the risk.
  6. Look Both Ways
    Even if the traffic appears to be coming from one direction, look both ways. Motorcycles and scooters often navigate through traffic and can come from unexpected directions.
  7. Watch Out for Motorbikes
    Motorbikes are common in Chiang Mai and can be more difficult to spot than cars. Be especially careful when crossing streets with heavy motorbike traffic.
  8. Use Pedestrian Bridges
    In some areas, pedestrian bridges are available. They are a safer alternative to crossing busy streets at ground level.
  9. Nighttime Caution
    Visibility is lower at night, so take extra care when crossing streets after dark. Wear light-colored clothing or something reflective if possible.
  10. Traffic Laws Awareness
    Be aware that traffic laws may not be as strictly enforced as in some other countries. Drivers might not always yield to pedestrians, so do not assume you have the right of way.

These tips will help you navigate the streets of Chiang Mai safely and confidently on foot, allowing you to explore the city at your own pace.

Chapter 3.7 – Driving in Chiang Mai

Driving in Chiang Mai can be quite an adventure! Here is a general overview:

Left-hand drive

Thailand follows the left-hand drive system, which might take some getting used to if you are from a country with right-hand drive.

Traffic and congestion

Chiang Mai's traffic can be busy, especially during rush hours and in popular tourist areas. However, it is generally less hectic than Bangkok. Be prepared for traffic jams and keep an eye out for motorbikes weaving in and out of cars.

Thai drivers

Thai drivers are generally courteous and patient, but their driving style may be different from what you are used to. You should be aware that it is clear that Thai drivers were not taught to "drive defensively! " They seem to believe that they can turn onto a road or change lanes without regard to others. Having said that, once you become accustomed to this style of driving, you will quickly get the hang of it. Keep in mind that honking is uncommon, and Thai drivers very rarely become aggressive or angry.

Motorbikes and motorcycles

Motorbikes and motorcycles are a popular mode of transportation in Chiang Mai. However, it is essential to exercise caution when driving or riding one, as accidents are more common. Always wear a helmet, follow traffic rules, and be aware of your surroundings.

Riding a bicycle

Bicycles are a great way to explore Chiang Mai's quieter streets and scenic areas. However, be cautious when riding alongside motorbikes and cars, as they may not always give you enough space. Stick to designated bicycle lanes when available, and always wear a helmet.

Overall, driving in Chiang Mai can be enjoyable as long as you remain alert, adapt to local driving customs, and follow traffic rules. Happy driving!

Traffic and How to get around

The traffic situation in Chiang Mai is currently challenging due to ongoing infrastructure work and increased vehicle usage. The city has significant congestion. The average commute time is around 25 minutes, with most people travelling by car. Additionally, the region has been grappling with environmental issues such as forest fires, which have further impacted traffic and air quality.

Motorbikes and public transportation options like buses are also common, but traffic inefficiency remains high, contributing to longer travel times and higher CO2 emissions.

If you are planning to travel in or around Chiang Mai, it’s advisable to check local news for updates on traffic conditions and plan your trips outside peak hours to avoid delays.

Driving in Chiang Mai in general, involves adhering to several traffic rules and being aware of local driving behaviours. Here are some key points to consider:

Traffic Rules and Regulations

  • Driving Side: In Thailand, vehicles drive on the left side of the road.
  • Speed Limits: Speed limits are generally set at 60 km/h in urban areas and up to 120 km/h on highways, but always look out for specific signs indicating speed limits as they can vary.
  • Helmet and Seat Belt Laws: Helmets are mandatory for motorbike riders and passengers. Seat belts are required for all passengers in a vehicle.
  • Road Signs: Familiarize yourself with common road signs such as stop signs, no entry signs, and speed limit indicators. The signs often include text in Thai, so learning some basic Thai terms for traffic signs can be beneficial.
  • Right of Way: Larger vehicles generally have the right of way. Be cautious at intersections and always give way to trucks and buses.

Common Driving Practices

  • Motorbikes: Motorbikes are a common mode of transportation and often weave through traffic. Be vigilant as they may not always adhere to traffic signals or lane discipline.
  • Pedestrians: Pedestrian crossings are not always respected. Pedestrians may dart across the road unpredictably, so always stay alert.
  • Overtaking: It is common for vehicles, including motorbikes, to overtake from both the left and right. Always check your mirrors and blind spots before changing lanes.
  • Traffic Signals: While there are traffic lights, enforcement can be lax. Do not assume that all drivers will stop at red lights or follow the signal order strictly.

Safety Tips

  • Defensive Driving: Given the unpredictable nature of traffic, drive defensively. Anticipate erratic manoeuvrers from other drivers and keep a safe distance from the vehicle in front.
  • Road Conditions: Be cautious of road conditions, especially after the rainy season when potholes and uneven surfaces are more common.
  • Interacting with Police: If stopped by traffic police, remain calm and respectful. Have your documentation ready, including your driver’s license, vehicle registration (copy of Blue Book – not the original), and, if you are a tourist, your passport. If a fine is payable, pay it and do not argue with the police.
  • Parking: Parking can be challenging in busy areas. Look for designated parking zones and be aware of parking fees, which vary depending on the area.

By understanding these rules and common practices, you can navigate the roads of Chiang Mai more safely and confidently.

Chapter 3.9 – Obtaining/Renewing a driver's license

Obtaining a license in Chiang Mai as a foreigner involves several steps and specific requirements. Here is a comprehensive guide to help you through the process:

Required Documents

  • Passport and Visa: A valid non-immigrant visa is typically required, but in some cases, a tourist visa might suffice.
  • Residence Certificate (TM30): Issued by the place where you are staying. This document proves your address in Thailand and is usually valid for 30 days.
  • Medical Certificate: Obtainable from any clinic or hospital. It must be dated within 30 days of your application.
  • Photographs: Two colour photos (1.5 inches x 2.36 inches).
  • Copies of Passport Pages: Include the first page, visa page, and TM.6 departure card.
  • International Driver's License or Home Country License: If applicable, to skip certain tests.

Steps to Get a License

  1. Prepare Your Documents: Ensure you have all necessary documents. Make copies and get everything in order before visiting the Department of Land Transport (DLT).
  2. Medical and Residence Certificates: Obtain these certificates before heading to the DLT.
  3. Visit the DLT Office: Located on Hang Dong Road in Chiang Mai. Arrive early and with all documents.
  4. Submit Documents and Tests:
    • Colour Blindness Test: Identify colours on a chart.
    • Reflex Test: Simulate driving by pressing brake and accelerator pedals.
    • Peripheral Test: Identify colours shown in your peripheral vision.
    • Depth Perception Test: Align a moving pin with a fixed one using control buttons.
    • Theory Test: For those without an international license, a computer-based test on Thai road rules.
    • Practical Driving Test: Demonstrate driving skills on a track at the test center.

Fees and Processing

  • Fees: Approximately 205 baht for a car license and 155 baht for a motorbike license. A protective cover for the license costs an additional 10 baht.
  • Processing Time: The entire process can usually be completed within a day if all documents are in order and tests are passed.

Additional Tips

  • Online Appointment: Book an appointment via the DLT Smart Queue app available for both iOS and Android to save time.
  • Language Assistance: If you don’t speak Thai, consider bringing a Thai-speaking friend or using an agent to assist with the process.

Renewal

The first license issued is temporary for two years. Afterward, it can be renewed for five years. Here are the steps to renew your license:

  1. Check your license expiration date: Make sure you know when your license expires to ensure you renew it on time.
  2. Gather required documents: You will need your current driving license, a medical certificate (issued within the last month), a passport-sized photo, and your passport or Thai ID card for identification.
  3. Visit the Department of Land Transport (DLT) office: Go to the nearest DLT office in Chiang Mai to submit your documents and begin the renewal process.
  4. Submit documents and pay fees: Submit your documents and pay any associated fees for the license renewal.
  5. Take a vision test: You may be required to complete a vision test to ensure you meet the necessary requirements for driving.
  6. Receive your renewed license: Once the process is complete, you will receive your renewed driver’s license.

Please note that the specific steps and requirements may vary depending on your personal circumstances, so it’s always a good idea to contact the local DLT office or consult their official website for the most up-to-date information.

Chapter 4.7 – Food – A general discussion

Food plays a significant role in Thai culture, influencing social interactions, traditions, and the country's identity. Here are some key aspects highlighting the importance of food in Thailand:

  • Social Interactions Thai meals are often communal affairs, with dishes placed in the center of the table for everyone to share. Eating alone is generally considered bad luck, so meals provide an opportunity for friends and family to come together, bond, and socialize.
  • Flavor Balance Thai cuisine strives for a harmonious balance of five primary flavors: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and spicy. This balance is essential to the overall dining experience and represents the Thai philosophy of maintaining harmony in life.
  • Cultural Identity Thai food is a source of national pride and plays a crucial role in the country's cultural identity. Traditional dishes and cooking techniques have been passed down through generations, preserving the culinary heritage and ensuring its continued relevance.
  • Festivals and Celebrations Food is central to many Thai festivals and celebrations, such as Songkran (Thai New Year) and Loy Krathong. Special dishes and treats are prepared and shared during these occasions, strengthening communal bonds and celebrating the country's rich cultural traditions.
  • Street Food Culture Thailand's vibrant street food scene is an integral part of daily life. Locals and visitors alike flock to street vendors for quick, affordable, and delicious meals, contributing to the bustling atmosphere of cities like Bangkok and Chiang Mai.
  • Culinary Tourism Thai cuisine has gained worldwide popularity, attracting tourists from around the globe to experience authentic dishes and learn about Thai culture through its flavors and culinary traditions.

In summary, food in Thailand is not just sustenance but an integral part of the country's cultural fabric, shaping social interactions, celebrations, and the nation's identity.

Chapter 4.11 – Negotiating Prices


Markets and Street Vendors

Initial Interaction:

  • Show Interest but Not Desperation: Express interest in the item but avoid showing that you’re overly eager to buy.
  • Ask for the Price: Inquire about the price first before making any counteroffer.

Making an Offer:

  • Start Lower: Offer a price that’s about 30-50% lower than the initial asking price. This leaves room for negotiation.
  • Bundle Items: If you’re buying multiple items, ask for a discount on the total purchase. Vendors are often more willing to give a better deal on larger sales.

Use Silence and Pause:

  • Wait for Their Response: After making an offer, remain silent and wait for the seller to respond. This can sometimes prompt them to lower the price without you needing to say more.
  • Pause Before Accepting: Even if the seller agrees to your offer, pause for a moment before accepting. This can sometimes lead to an even better deal.

Shops and Boutiques

Ask for Discounts:

  • Inquire Politely: Ask if there are any discounts or promotions, especially if you’re buying multiple items.
  • Cash Payments: Sometimes, paying in cash can get you a better deal since it saves the seller credit card processing fees.

Leverage Competitors:

  • Mention Alternatives: Politely mention if you’ve seen similar items at a lower price elsewhere. This can encourage the seller to match or beat the competitor’s price.

Final Tips

  • Stay Calm and Patient: Keep the negotiation process friendly and relaxed. Losing your temper or becoming impatient can negatively impact the outcome.
  • Know When to Stop: Once you reach a fair price, stop negotiating. Pushing too hard can lead to the seller retracting their offer.
  • Practice Makes Perfect: The more you practice negotiating, the better you’ll become at reading situations and achieving favourable outcomes.

Chapter 4.16 – Security

Chiang Mai has a well-deserved reputation as a safe and friendly place. However, ensuring your security and safety while in Chiang Mai involves being aware of your surroundings and taking precautions typical of any urban environment. Here are some tips to help you stay safe in Chiang Mai:

General Safety Tips

  • Stay Informed: Stay updated on local news and events, including any safety advisories or warnings.
  • Emergency Contacts: Keep a list of emergency contacts handy, including local police, ambulance services, and your country's embassy or consulate.
  • Travel Insurance: Consider purchasing travel insurance that covers medical emergencies, trip cancellations, and other unexpected incidents.
  • Stay Connected: Inform someone trustworthy about your travel plans and whereabouts, especially if you are exploring remote areas or engaging in outdoor activities.

Personal Security

  • Avoid Risky Areas: Be cautious in unfamiliar or poorly lit areas, especially at night. Stick to well-lit and populated areas, particularly when walking alone.
  • Secure Valuables: Keep your belongings secure and avoid displaying expensive items like jewellery, cameras, or electronics in public.
  • Use Caution with Strangers: Be cautious when interacting with strangers, especially in nightlife areas. Avoid accepting drinks from people you do not know well.

Transportation Safety

  • Use Licensed Taxis: Use licensed taxis or reputable ride-hailing services like Grab for transportation. Avoid unmarked or unofficial taxis.
  • Be Vigilant on Public Transportation: Keep an eye on your belongings when using buses, tuk-tuks, or songthaews (red trucks), especially during crowded times.
  • Stay Alert While Driving: If you are renting a motorbike or driving a car, stay alert and follow traffic laws. Be cautious of other drivers, especially in busy areas.

Health and Medical Safety

  • Stay Hydrated: Drink plenty of water, especially in hot and humid weather, to prevent dehydration.
  • Mosquito Protection: Use insect repellent and wear long sleeves and pants to protect against mosquito-borne illnesses like dengue fever.
  • Seek Medical Attention if Needed: If you feel unwell or experience any medical concerns, seek medical attention promptly. Chiang Mai has modern medical facilities and hospitals to provide quality healthcare services.

Cultural Sensitivity

  • Respect Local Customs: Be respectful of local customs, traditions, and religious practices. Dress modestly when visiting temples or religious sites, and remove your shoes before entering.
  • Language Barrier: Learn some basic Thai phrases or carry a phrasebook to help with communication, especially in rural areas where English proficiency may be limited.

Natural Disasters

  • Rainy Season Precautions: During the rainy season (May to October), be prepared for heavy rainfall and potential flooding. Stay informed about weather forecasts and heed any warnings from local authorities.
  • Earthquake Awareness: While earthquakes are rare in Chiang Mai, it is a good idea to familiarize yourself with safety procedures in case of seismic activity.

Chapter 4.18 – Other Immigration Requirements for Expats

In addition to visa extensions and renewals, there are several other immigration requirements that expats in Thailand should be aware of:

  • TM 30: This is a form that must be submitted by landlords or property owners to the Immigration Bureau within 24 hours of a foreign national's arrival at their property. Expats should ensure that their landlord or property owner submits this form on their behalf.
  • 90-Day Reporting: Expats who hold a long-term visa or extension of stay must report their address to the Immigration Bureau every 90 days. This can be done in person, by mail, or online.
  • Re-Entry Permit: If you plan to leave Thailand and return during the validity of your visa or extension of stay, you will need to obtain a re-entry permit before leaving the country.
  • Work Permit: If you plan to work in Thailand, you will need to obtain a work permit from the Ministry of Labor.
  • Thai Bank Account: Many expats choose to open a Thai bank account to manage their finances in Thailand. Some banks may require a work permit or other documentation to open an account.

It is important to stay informed about these and other immigration requirements to ensure that you remain in compliance with Thai law.
